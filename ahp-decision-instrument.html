Step three

Pick a criterion, then compare the options against each other on that criterion alone. Tilt the beam toward the option you prefer on this criterion. For something like cost, that means tilting toward the cheaper option, since lower cost is the thing you want. Judging one lens at a time is what keeps the comparisons honest.

Step five

How the instrument works

The Analytic Hierarchy Process breaks a hard choice into a tree, gathers human judgment as simple two-at-a-time comparisons, and lets linear algebra turn those comparisons into weights. Its real gift is not the ranking. It is that it measures how consistent your own judgments are and shows you where they contradict each other.

The four moves

01
Structure. State one goal. List the criteria that matter and the options on the table. To move fast, load a template or describe the decision and let the assistant suggest a structure you can edit. The 3D tree on the left is this hierarchy made visible.
02
Weigh criteria. Compare criteria two at a time using the balance beam. The beam tilts toward the heavier criterion. You answer many small questions instead of one impossible one.
03
Score options. For each criterion in turn, compare the options against each other on that criterion only.
04
Synthesise. Each option's global score is the sum across criteria of (criterion weight × option score on that criterion). The model does this for you.

What consistency really measures

If you say coffee beats tea, and tea beats water, you should not also say water beats coffee. That loop is the intuition. What AHP actually measures is stronger than that ordering: it checks whether your judgments agree in scale, so that if coffee is 2x tea and tea is 3x water, coffee should be about 6x water. It computes the principal eigenvector of your comparison matrix to get the weights, then measures how far your judgments drift from that perfect consistency as a consistency ratio.

  • Under 0.10 — coherent enough to trust.
  • 0.10 to 0.20 — usable, but worth revisiting the flagged pair.
  • Over 0.20 — the judgments are contradicting themselves. The instrument names the single comparison most responsible and suggests the value that would resolve it.
The point is not to score a perfect zero. A little inconsistency is human and even healthy. A high ratio is a signal that the group has a genuine disagreement or a fuzzy criterion hiding underneath the numbers. That conversation is the value.

When you need the Analytic Network Process instead

AHP assumes the criteria are independent. Often they are not. Reputation lifts sales, sales fund reputation. Cost depends on quality, quality depends on the budget you set. When criteria feed back into each other, or options influence the criteria, a strict top-down tree distorts the answer.

The Analytic Network Process generalises AHP. It replaces the tree with a network where any element can influence any other, captures those influences in a supermatrix, and raises that matrix to powers until the feedback loops settle into stable weights. Use AHP when the criteria stand alone. Reach for ANP when the honest answer to "does this depend on that" is yes. This instrument models AHP. If your map of the decision starts growing arrows that point back on themselves, that is the signal to graduate to ANP.

For the facilitator

Running the session

Your job is not to supply answers. It is to keep the comparisons honest, keep the loudest voice from setting every weight, and turn each spike of inconsistency into a real conversation. The model handles the math. You handle the room.

Before the room arrives

  • Enter the goal, criteria, and options yourself, agreed with the sponsor in advance. Walking in with a blank structure burns the first twenty minutes on wording.
  • Keep criteria to between four and seven. More than seven and the pairwise count balloons and attention drops.
  • Load the example once so the group sees a finished model, then reset to neutral before live judgments begin.
  • Decide your elicitation method up front. Either poll the group per comparison and enter the consensus, or take a show of hands, or let a sub-group own each criterion.

Handling the moments that come up

The consistency card turns red. Do not just apply the suggested fix. Read the flagged pair aloud. Usually it means two people were picturing different things, or a criterion means different things to different people. Name that, then re-judge.
One person dominates every comparison. Switch to silent polling for a few rounds. Ask each person for their ratio before anyone speaks, then combine the numbers with the geometric mean, multiply them together and take the root, and round to the nearest point on the scale. The geometric mean is the mathematically sound way to merge AHP judgments because it preserves the reciprocal relationship, and it stops the senior voice from setting the number on its own.
"The model picked the wrong answer." Good. That reaction is information. Either a judgment does not reflect real belief, so fix the judgment, or the group's gut is reacting to something not yet in the criteria. Add the missing criterion rather than overriding the math.
The group wants to skip pairwise and just rate everything. Remind them that direct rating is exactly what hides inconsistency. The two-at-a-time discipline is the whole point. It is slower because it is catching the contradictions a rating scale would bury.

Closing the session

  • State the result as "the model derived" not "the model decided." The group owns the call.
  • Record the final consistency ratios alongside the ranking. They are the audit trail for why this decision was sound.
  • If any criterion clearly depends on another, note it for a follow-up. That is the flag that the next version of this decision may need the Analytic Network Process.
